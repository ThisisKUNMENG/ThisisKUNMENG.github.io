      Forecasting

      1 Introduction to forecasting

      Types of Forecasts

      • Qualitative Methods
        • When quantitative data is difficult to obtain
        • When subjective / expert knowledge is very important
        • Long-term forecasts
        • For example: new product sales
        • Delphi Method, Jury of executive opinion, Sales force composite, Consumer market study
      • Time Series Methods
        • When historical quantitative data is available
        • When the past is relevant for the future
        • For example: sales of lawn mowers
        • Moving average, Weighted moving average, Exponential smoothing, Trend analysis, Seasonality analysis, Decomposition
      • Causal Methods
        • When historical quantitative data is available
        • When the dependent variable is likely influenced by other known variables
        • For example: heating system & field service force; temperature & demand for soft drinks
        • Simple regression analysis, Multiple regression analysis

      Steps to Forecasting

      1. Determine the use of the forecast - what are you trying to obtain?
      2. Identify the items you want to forecast
      3. Determine the time horizon
      4. Select and build model
      5. Gather data
      6. Validate model
      7. Forecast

      From CMA to Unseasoned Value

      1. Calculate seasonal ratio, which is demand(sales) divides CMA
      2. Calculate seasonal index, which is average of seasonal ratio of the corresponding season
      3. Divide the demand by the seasonal index to get the unseasoned value

      From Unseasoned Value To Forecast

      1. Use the unseasoned value to do a linear regression
      2. Use the linear regression coefficients to do trend-based forecast
      3. Multiply the trend-based forecast and the seasonal index to get seasonalized trend

      Locations and Functions of Inventory

      • Inventory classification by location
        • Raw materials: goods received from supplier
        • Work-in-progress: at some point within the operations process
        • Finished goods: goods ready for dispatch to the customer
        • Suppliers(Raw materials,30%) → Operation(Work-in-progress, 40%) → Customers(Finished goods, 30%), percentage is a rough estimate
      • Inventory classification by function
        • Buffer and safety: To cope with randomness in demand and supply
        • Cycles: Multiple products from one operation → keep supply while other products are produced
        • Decoupling: Match the rate of processing at different points in the production processes
        • Quantity game: To enable bulk and other discounts
        • Pipeline/movement: Compensate for transportation and distribution times

      ABC-Analysis

      • Each item in inventory has different volume
      1. Calculate the item’s volume percentage of all the inventory
      2. Sort the item based on volume from high to low
      3. Calculate cumulative volume percentage
      4. A - 20%, roughly accounts for 80% of total volume; B - 30%, roughly accounts for 10-15% of total volume; C - 50%, roughly accounts for 5-10% of total volume
      • A and B needs careful thought

      4 Uncertainty and Discrete Demand

      Safety Stock

      • EPQ has a fundamental assumption that we know the demand, but in reality this is not true
      • To deal with uncertainty: safety stock, which is to increase stock during lead time
      1. We can use data from the past to calculate expected demand with probability (DDLT Distribution)
      2. Do a scenario analysis (alternative ROP)
      3. Calculate expected additional cost
        1. If DDLT>ROPDDLT > ROPDDLT>ROP, then additional cost is (DDLT−ROP)⋅Stockoutcost⋅numberoforders(DDLT-ROP) \cdot Stockout \; cost \cdot number \; of \; orders(DDLT−ROP)⋅Stockoutcost⋅numberoforders
        2. If DDLT<ROPDDLT < ROPDDLT<ROP, then addition cost is (ROP−DDLT)⋅Holdingcostperyear(ROP-DDLT) \cdot Holding \; cost \; per \; year(ROP−DDLT)⋅Holdingcostperyear
      4. Choose the least expected additional cost

      6 Inventory Management in Practice

      Existing Inventory Management Approaches

      • Based on reorder point
      • time is constant or variable
      • order quantity is constant or variable
      • (r, q) approach: t varies and q constant
        • applied when demand is not very stable
      • (r, T) approach: t varies and q varies
        • when reached reorder point, restock to the target inventory level

      • (t, q) approach: t constant and q constant

      • (t, T) approach: t constant and q varies

      2 Aggregated Planning

      Basics of Aggregated Planning

      • Making sure that we can fulfill expected demand (make or buy)
      • It does not focus on single products, but on product groups
      • It’s about machines, plans and human resources
      • Focus on inventory holding cost, human resources, and subcontract cost

      Chase Strategy & Level Strategy

      • Chase Strategy: Chasing the forecast demand by adopting human resources (hiring and firing), but it has social and economic challenges
      • Level Strategy: Looking at the maximum demand and just hire to the maximum
      • Two strategies do not take into account inventory and subcontracting

      3 Material Requirements Planning (MRP)

      Fundamentals

      • MPS and MRP are similar. MPS looks at products and MRP is not only about products, but products, sub assemblies and components. MRP is more detailed.
      • The complexity comes from thousands of parts which go into one product
      • Bill of material: Capture the structure of a product
        • Multi-level, Single-level, Flattened
        • Multi-level: BOM Tree, list
      • We can use BOM to do MRP

      Procedure

      1. Gross demand calculation
        • Look at each and every part and see the gross demand
      2. Net demand calculation
        • Take existing inventory and incoming deliveries into account and see the net demand
      3. Lead shifting
        • Calculate lead time adjusted net demand
      4. Lot size optimization
        • Calculate lot size optimized orders (discussed in inventory management)

      5 Lean Manufacturing & Just-In-Time Management

      Just-in-Time Philosophy

      • Reduce waste and variability
      • Dimensions
      1. Waste
        • Defective products, time on hand (waiting), overproduction, transportation, movement, processing, stock at hand
      2. Workplace organization (5S)
        • Sort, Set in order, Shine, Standardize, Sustain
      3. Standardization
      4. Kaizen
        • Continuous improvement
        • bottom up innovation approach
      5. Quality (the heart of JIT)
        • Avoid errors (Poka-Yoke), 100% in-line control (Jidoka), empowerment, preventive maintenance, visualization
      6. Layout and material flow
        • Batch production, flow production, takt (synchronized production with predefined time segments), flexible employee assignment
      7. Pull management
        • Pull principle: put demand first, demand drives production
        • Kanban system (mandatory reading)
      8. Supplier integration

      Bring it together: push and pull

      • Push: Demand Forecast →\rightarrow→ MRP →\rightarrow→ Production
      • Pull: Demand →\rightarrow→ Production
      • Order Penetration Point (OPP)
        • Example: Steakhouse
          • Pull: Cook and serve after ordering
          • Push: Breede and Butcher in advance
          • Raw beef is stored in fridge, the fridge is the order penetration point

      Supply chain fundamentals

      • The company at the center of the supply chain is often what we focus on and we will look at its operations.
      • Suppliers that directly supply goods to company are called first tier suppliers (sub-assembly). And there are second (component) tier suppliers, third tier suppliers (raw material).
      • Customer side is the same. There are first tier customers (wholesaler) whom the company directly sell its goods to. And there second tier customers (retailer) and third tier customers (end consumer).
      • Suppliers are upstream and customers are downstream.
      • Inbound activities means the logistics activity between first tier suppliers and the company**.** Outbound activities means the logistics activity between first tier customers and the company**.**

      2 Postponement

      • many product configurations (different power cord, different phone memory, etc.)can be a challenge for demand and supply chain.
      • Without postponement
        • Example: Produce alarm clock in Asia and ship to Germany to a warehouse, then distribute inside Europe.
        • This means the product configuration point is in Asia, and will ship many product configurations.
        • Advantage: Cheap labor and simple
        • Challenge: If a boom in Switzerland and a decline in Germany occurs, total demand is the same but it will take a lot of time to change the supply (due to transportation time). This means no flexibility.
      • With postponement
        • Still, produce alarm clock in Asia, but not attaching the power cords to alarm clock itself. In the German warehouse then we attach the power cord.
        • We can attach more Swiss power cords and solve the challenge above.

      Impact of postponement

      • Without postponement
        • There is a product with different configurations.
        • Each product configuration has a demand based on a normal distribution.
        1. We can calculate the order quantity QQQ and associated costs ccc, because each product configuration is treated as a different product. (see Periodic Order Management with Target Inventory Level)
        2. sum up the overall order quantity and costs.
      • With postponement
        1. Calculate the overall μ\muμ and σ\sigmaσ for the product.
          • μ=∑i=1nμi\mu = \sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\mu_iμ=i=1∑n​μi​
          • σ=∑i=1nσi2\sigma = \sqrt{\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\sigma_i^2}σ=i=1∑n​σi2​​
          • where μi\mu_iμi​ and σi\sigma_iσi​ is the mean and std. for each of the product configuration
          • we assume that each product configuration’s distribution is independent of each other
        2. Calculate the order quantity QQQ and associated costs ccc
      • With postponement, QQQ and ccc are lower compared to without postponement.
      • Advantages of postponement (more flexibility to uncertain demand)
        • neutralization effect (like a boom in Switzerland and a decline in Germany)
        • lower deviation
        • small inventory level and lower cost
      • Disadvantages of postponement
        • Need to invest in the final assembly place
        • more complex (more manufacturing places)
        • Responsibilities are complex and unclear
        • Product modification: products may be modified to be suited for postponement, which costs money

      The impact of order quantity

      • Each retailer has different order quantity and period, so wholesaler has fluctuate demand.
      • Each wholesaler has different order quantity and period, so manufacturer has more fluctuate demand.
      • High order quantities lead to high fluctuations.
      • To avoid high order quantities:
        • Automation to reduce cost per order
        • Coordinated deliveries, delivery cost reduced
        • Coordinated orders, which asks some customers to place orders in periods that other customers do not place orders

      The impact of price fluctuation

      • If there is a discount from the supplier, we can buy supply in advance, inventory costs and discounts need to be compared to get a maximum order in advance so that inventory costs are not too high. Buy supply in advance can reduce ordering cost.
      • This increases the supplier’s demand fluctuation.
      • To avoid fluctuation:
        • Promotion Profitability: This increases revenue due to selling more, but supply chain and corresponding costs needs to be considered.
        • Intensity: A right intensity for a sales promotion
        • Alternatives: think about other means to keep demand stable
        • Distinguish between order and delivery, decouple the discount from delivery to get a stable demand

      5 Supply Chain Decision Making on Sample Data

      • Bootstrapping is used to derive sample estimates, estimating the bootstrapping properties of an estimator (demand means)
        • confidence intervals, standard errors…
      • Advantages of bootstrapping: nonparametric approach to statistical inference
        • does not rely on assumptions
        • does not need normal distribution
        • does not need a large sample size
        • can be applied in any case

      Bootstrapping to estimate confidence intervals

      1. Bootstrap sample: choose 10 (or some other number) values from original distribution
        • In Excel, use the formula INDEX(Dataset, RANDBETWEEN(1, 10))
      2. Create 1000 different bootstrap samples
      3. Calculate each bootstrap sample’s mean, and sort them
      4. If going for 80% CI, then select 80% of the sorted mean in the middle

      • We can bootstrap the delta output of the machine (use data table in Excel) and estimate CI

        • Price reductions
        • Capacity management (booking classes and overbooking)

      4 Product Bundling

      • To sell different products in one product bundle (like software, automotive)
      • Why bundle?
        • Simplicity, comfort
        • Cross-selling
        • Differentiation
        • Cost reduction (economies of scale)
        • Capture full WTP (willingness to pay), thus increasing revenue

      Separate Pricing

      • Each product will be priced individually
      • Each customer (segment) has different willingness to pay for each product
      • Each product has a cost
      1. Calculate the profit at each price point for each product
      2. Add the maximum profit of each product to get the total profit

      Pure Bundling

      • Buy a bundle or not buy a bundle, customer can not buy each product separately
      • Bundle price is the sum of the willingness to pay of each product
      1. Calculate the profit at each price point for bundle
      2. Get the maximum profit of bundling to get the total profit

      Mixed Bundling

      • Allow customers to either buy bundle or buy single product. They have full flexibility.
      • Goal is to get the best price for each product and bundle to maximize profit.
      1. Calculate Consumer Surplus: CS=WTP−PCS=WTP-PCS=WTP−P
      2. For the price option, customer will buy the option that will create the largest CS
      3. Calculate profit based on the customer’s choice
      4. Use solver to get the maximize profit
        • We have used Smooth Optimization Problem in the solver, which the function is smooth
        • But in mixed bundling, function is not smooth, which is Non-Smooth Optimization Problem (For example, IF, ABS, MAX, MIN …)
        • So Excel needs to use a different strategy to solve called evolutionary solver
        1. Create constraints: Price should be smaller than the maximum bundle price and be larger than 100 or something.
        2. select Evolutionary solving method
        3. The last step is to see whether there is price reversal, which is whether individual product’s price is higher than the bundle price
      • The maximum CSCSCS should be zero for each customer.

      Two-Part Tariff

      • There is an entry fee (one-time access fee) and usage fee (per-unit price)
        • For example, golf club, credit card…
      • In which case two-part tariff can be applied
        • Firm has market power
        • Consumer is uncertain about their demand
        • Firm can control access
      • Designing the two-part tariff to maximize revenue
      1. There is several customer segments and they have different willingness to pay for different units of product
      2. Calculate the total price for different units of product using the fixed price and unit price
      3. Calculate consumer surplus CSCSCS in each case, each customer will make the choice that will maximize their CSCSCS (Note that if all of CSCSCS is less than 000, the customer will not buy any product)
      4. Calculate the revenue for each customer and get the total profit
      5. Use Excel solver to optimize the Two-Part Tariff to maximize profit
