      Forecasting

      1 Introduction to forecasting

      Types of Forecasts

      • Qualitative Methods
        • When quantitative data is difficult to obtain
        • When subjective / expert knowledge is very important
        • Long-term forecasts
        • For example: new product sales
        • Delphi Method, Jury of executive opinion, Sales force composite, Consumer market study
      • Time Series Methods
        • When historical quantitative data is available
        • When the past is relevant for the future
        • For example: sales of lawn mowers
        • Moving average, Weighted moving average, Exponential smoothing, Trend analysis, Seasonality analysis, Decomposition
      • Causal Methods
        • When historical quantitative data is available
        • When the dependent variable is likely influenced by other known variables
        • For example: heating system & field service force; temperature & demand for soft drinks
        • Simple regression analysis, Multiple regression analysis

      Steps to Forecasting

      1. Determine the use of the forecast - what are you trying to obtain?
      2. Identify the items you want to forecast
      3. Determine the time horizon
      4. Select and build model
      5. Gather data
      6. Validate model
      7. Forecast

      From CMA to Unseasoned Value

      1. Calculate seasonal ratio, which is demand(sales) divides CMA
      2. Calculate seasonal index, which is average of seasonal ratio of the corresponding season
      3. Divide the demand by the seasonal index to get the unseasoned value

      From Unseasoned Value To Forecast

      1. Use the unseasoned value to do a linear regression
      2. Use the linear regression coefficients to do trend-based forecast
      3. Multiply the trend-based forecast and the seasonal index to get seasonalized trend

      Locations and Functions of Inventory

      • Inventory classification by location
        • Raw materials: goods received from supplier
        • Work-in-progress: at some point within the operations process
        • Finished goods: goods ready for dispatch to the customer
        • Suppliers(Raw materials,30%) → Operation(Work-in-progress, 40%) → Customers(Finished goods, 30%), percentage is a rough estimate
      • Inventory classification by function
        • Buffer and safety: To cope with randomness in demand and supply
        • Cycles: Multiple products from one operation → keep supply while other products are produced
        • Decoupling: Match the rate of processing at different points in the production processes
        • Quantity game: To enable bulk and other discounts
        • Pipeline/movement: Compensate for transportation and distribution times

      ABC-Analysis

      • Each item in inventory has different volume
      1. Calculate the item’s volume percentage of all the inventory
      2. Sort the item based on volume from high to low
      3. Calculate cumulative volume percentage
      4. A - 20%, roughly accounts for 80% of total volume; B - 30%, roughly accounts for 10-15% of total volume; C - 50%, roughly accounts for 5-10% of total volume
      • A and B needs careful thought

      4 Uncertainty and Discrete Demand

      Safety Stock

      • EPQ has a fundamental assumption that we know the demand, but in reality this is not true
      • To deal with uncertainty: safety stock, which is to increase stock during lead time
      1. We can use data from the past to calculate expected demand with probability (DDLT Distribution)
      2. Do a scenario analysis (alternative ROP)
      3. Calculate expected additional cost
        1. If DDLT>ROPDDLT > ROPDDLT>ROP, then additional cost is (DDLT−ROP)⋅Stockoutcost⋅numberoforders(DDLT-ROP) \cdot Stockout \; cost \cdot number \; of \; orders(DDLT−ROP)⋅Stockoutcost⋅numberoforders
        2. If DDLT<ROPDDLT < ROPDDLT<ROP, then addition cost is (ROP−DDLT)⋅Holdingcostperyear(ROP-DDLT) \cdot Holding \; cost \; per \; year(ROP−DDLT)⋅Holdingcostperyear
      4. Choose the least expected additional cost

      6 Inventory Management in Practice

      Existing Inventory Management Approaches

      • Based on reorder point
      • time is constant or variable
      • order quantity is constant or variable
      • (r, q) approach: t varies and q constant
        • applied when demand is not very stable
      • (r, T) approach: t varies and q varies
        • when reached reorder point, restock to the target inventory level
      • periodic inventory management: order periodically (t is constant)
      • (t, q) approach: t constant and q constant
        • when having stable demand
      • (t, T) approach: t constant and q varies
        • order to meet the target inventory level

      2 Aggregated Planning

      Basics of Aggregated Planning

      • Making sure that we can fulfill expected demand (make or buy)
      • It does not focus on single products, but on product groups
      • It’s about machines, plans and human resources
      • Focus on inventory holding cost, human resources, and subcontract cost

      Chase Strategy & Level Strategy

      • Chase Strategy: Chasing the forecast demand by adopting human resources (hiring and firing), but it has social and economic challenges
      • Level Strategy: Looking at the maximum demand and just hire to the maximum
      • Two strategies do not take into account inventory and subcontracting

      3 Material Requirements Planning (MRP)

      Fundamentals

      • MPS and MRP are similar. MPS looks at products and MRP is not only about products, but products, sub assemblies and components. MRP is more detailed.
      • The complexity comes from thousands of parts which go into one product
      • Bill of material: Capture the structure of a product
        • Multi-level, Single-level, Flattened
        • Multi-level: BOM Tree, list
      • We can use BOM to do MRP

      Procedure

      1. Gross demand calculation
        • Look at each and every part and see the gross demand
      2. Net demand calculation
        • Take existing inventory and incoming deliveries into account and see the net demand
      3. Lead shifting
        • Calculate lead time adjusted net demand
      4. Lot size optimization
        • Calculate lot size optimized orders (discussed in inventory management)

      5 Lean Manufacturing & Just-In-Time Management

      Just-in-Time Philosophy

      • Reduce waste and variability
      • Dimensions
      1. Waste
        • Defective products, time on hand (waiting), overproduction, transportation, movement, processing, stock at hand
      2. Workplace organization (5S)
        • Sort, Set in order, Shine, Standardize, Sustain
      3. Standardization
      4. Kaizen
        • Continuous improvement
        • bottom up innovation approach
      5. Quality (the heart of JIT)
        • Avoid errors (Poka-Yoke), 100% in-line control (Jidoka), empowerment, preventive maintenance, visualization
      6. Layout and material flow
        • Batch production, flow production, takt (synchronized production with predefined time segments), flexible employee assignment
      7. Pull management
        • Pull principle: put demand first, demand drives production
        • Kanban system (mandatory reading)
      8. Supplier integration

      Bring it together: push and pull

      • Push: Demand Forecast →\rightarrow→ MRP →\rightarrow→ Production
      • Pull: Demand →\rightarrow→ Production
      • Order Penetration Point (OPP)
        • Example: Steakhouse
          • Pull: Cook and serve after ordering
          • Push: Breede and Butcher in advance
          • Raw beef is stored in fridge, the fridge is the order penetration point
        • OOP is where push and pull come together
        • OOP is different in different industries
          • Manufacturing process: development, production components, final assembly, configuration, delivery
          • Engineer to order - ETO: building a house
          • Make to order - MTO: CNC machines
          • Assemble to order -ATO: cars
          • Configure to order - CTO: ERP systems
          • Make to stock - MTS: smartphones
